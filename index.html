📋 Triage Steps — Read Ahead of Time
  1. Observe — What are you seeing? When did it start? Is the ferret eating and moving normally?
  2. Find the card — Look it up below or use the search bar. Read the full card.
  3. Contact the board if needed — Green items: reach out if it's your first time or you're unsure. Yellow and above: always contact the board before or while acting.
  4. Act — Board will tell you whether to handle at home, have someone come over, or head to the vet.
  5. Write it down — Note what happened and what you did. Let the board know so we can track patterns.

⚡ Know Before It Happens
🩸
Low blood sugar (insulinoma): Rub Karo syrup or honey on the gums. Follow with protein once alert. Never pour liquid into an unconscious ferret. Call board. Seizing or unconscious → emergency vet immediately.
🌡️
Heat stroke (above 80°F): Move to a cool area. Apply cool damp cloth to paws and armpits. Do NOT use cold water. Emergency vet immediately.
🚫
Swallowed rubber or foam: Repeated vomiting, no stool, painful belly → emergency vet now. Keep all foam, rubber, and soft plastics away from ferrets at all times.
💊
Keep on hand: Karo syrup or honey · Benadryl cream · Ferret Lax · Chicken baby food · Styptic powder
✅ Is This Normal? — Don't Panic
😴
Dead sleep — Ferrets sleep so deeply they look lifeless and limp. Gently rustle them — if they wake within 30–60 seconds, they're fine.
😄
Dooking and bouncing — A happy clucking sound with erratic bouncing is the "weasel war dance." It means your ferret is excited and playing.
💨
Musky smell — Some odor is completely normal. A sudden strong change in smell can signal illness.
🤧
A few sneezes — Normal, especially after digging in litter. Persistent sneezing with discharge is not normal.
💤
Sleeping 14–18 hours a day — Completely normal. Ferrets are most active at dawn and dusk and sleep a lot in between.
😬
Hissing — Normal when startled or annoyed. Not a sign of aggression toward you.
💩
Slightly soft stools — Can happen after a diet or environment change. Monitor 24 hours. If no blood and it resolves, usually fine.
📅 Seasonal Reminders
Summer
Ferrets cannot handle heat. Keep their space below 75–80°F. Never leave near a sunny window or in a car. Heat stroke can kill in minutes.
Summer
Confirm the ferret is on monthly heartworm prevention. Contact board if unsure.
Winter
Ferrets may sleep much more than usual and be hard to rouse — this is called torpor and is normal. Monitor closely as it can look like illness.
Winter
Ferrets can catch human flu. Wash hands before handling and avoid close contact when you're sick.
Spring
Adrenal disease flare-ups are more common as daylight hours increase. Watch for new hair loss or behavior changes.
Always
Keep foam, rubber toys, shoe insoles, and soft plastics out of reach. Swallowing these is a leading preventable cause of death.
