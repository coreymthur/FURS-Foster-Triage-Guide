Before you do anything — follow these steps
  1. Observe and note — What are the exact symptoms? When did they start? Is the ferret eating, drinking, moving normally?
  2. Check this guide — Find the matching condition. Read the full card.
  3. Call or text the board if needed — For green items, reach out if you're unsure or it's your first time. For everything yellow and above, always contact a board member before or while acting. A photo or 10-second video is worth a thousand words.
  4. Act based on guidance — The board member will confirm whether to handle at home, come over, or go to the vet.
  5. Document it — Note what happened, what you did, and how the ferret responded. Tell the board so we can track patterns.
⚠️ For RED emergencies: act first, call while driving. Do not wait for a callback.

Nail Trimming
🟢 Foster can handle independently

Signs to look for

Nails clicking on hard surfaces, curling, catching on fabric.

What to do

Call a board member first time for a video walkthrough. Use small pet or human nail clippers. Trim just the clear tip — avoid the pink quick (blood vessel). If you hit the quick and it bleeds, apply styptic powder or cornstarch with light pressure.

Board assistance (optional)

Yes — first time. After you're trained, you can do this independently.

When to escalate

If nails are severely curled into the foot pad or paw is swollen/infected → 🔵 Schedule vet.

Ear Cleaning (Mild Brown Buildup)
🟢 Foster can handle independently

Signs to look for

Moderate brown waxy buildup in ears, no odor, ferret not scratching excessively.

What to do

Use a ferret-safe or kitten ear cleaner. Dampen a cotton ball — never insert cotton swabs deep into the canal. Wipe visible wax gently. Contact a board member before cleaning if you've never done it.

Board assistance (optional)

Yes — first time or if unsure.

When to escalate

If buildup is dark/black and crumbly with head shaking or scratching → likely ear mites. See that card (🔵).

Mild Low Blood Sugar Episode (Known Insulinomic Ferret)
🟢 Foster can handle independently

Signs to look for

Glassy stare, drooling, pawing at mouth, wobbly hind legs, seems dazed or slow. Ferret is still conscious.

What to do

Immediately rub a small amount of Karo syrup, honey, or Nutrical on the gums. Do NOT give liquid to an unconscious ferret. Follow with a small protein meal (chicken baby food, softened kibble) once alert — the sugar spike will be followed by a crash without protein. Keep ferret warm. Call board member right away.

Board assistance (optional)

Yes — immediately. Even if ferret recovers, we need to know.

⚠️ Every episode must be reported — frequency tells us if medical management needs adjusting.

When to escalate

If ferret does not respond within 2–3 minutes, loses consciousness, or has a full seizure → 🔴 Emergency vet immediately.

Mast Cell Tumor — Small, Non-Ulcerated (Known)
🟢 Foster can handle independently

Signs to look for

Small (under 1cm), circular, slightly raised reddish bump on skin. May wax and wane. Ferret mostly unbothered by it.

What to do

Apply Benadryl cream (diphenhydramine topical) 2–3x daily if ferret is itching at it. This reduces histamine production and irritation. Take a photo and send to board member. Do NOT attempt to freeze with OTC wart removers — this can damage tissue.

Board assistance (optional)

Yes — send a photo for documentation. Board member will advise whether it needs vet evaluation.

⚠️ If the tumor ulcerates, grows rapidly, or becomes infected — escalate to 🔵 or 🔴.

When to escalate

If new, unknown lump: treat as 🔵 until identified by vet.

Hairball / Minor Vomiting (Isolated Incident)
🟢 Foster can handle independently

Signs to look for

Single vomiting episode, no blood, ferret alert afterward and eating normally.

What to do

Offer fresh water and monitor. Give a small amount of Ferret Lax or Petromalt (hairball remedy) per package instructions. Keep food available.

Board assistance (optional)

Report within the day.

When to escalate

Repeated vomiting, blood, lethargy, or not eating → 🔴 Emergency. Ferrets can block quickly.

Mild Diarrhea (Single Day, No Blood)
🟢 Foster can handle independently

Signs to look for

Loose or soft stools, no blood, ferret otherwise alert. May follow a diet change.

What to do

Ensure fresh water access. Avoid diet changes. Monitor closely. Keep a photo of the stool to share with board.

Board assistance (optional)

Yes — report same day with a description or photo of stool.

When to escalate

Blood in stool, black tarry stool, or diarrhea lasting more than 24–48 hrs → 🔵 or 🔴 depending on severity.

Minor Cuts or Scrapes
🟢 Foster can handle independently

Signs to look for

Small wound from play or cage. Minor bleeding that stops within a few minutes.

What to do

Clean with dilute chlorhexidine solution or saline. Apply gentle pressure with clean gauze if still bleeding. Do NOT use hydrogen peroxide — too harsh for ferret tissue. Keep the area clean and watch for infection.

Board assistance (optional)

Yes — notify and send a photo.

When to escalate

Deep puncture wounds, wounds that won't stop bleeding, or signs of infection (swelling, discharge, heat) → 🔵 or 🔴.

Sneezing (Occasional, No Other Symptoms)
🟢 Foster can handle independently

Signs to look for

Occasional sneezing, no nasal discharge, eating and active normally.

What to do

Check for dust in bedding or litter. Switch to unscented, low-dust litter. Monitor.

Board assistance (optional)

Only if sneezing increases or other symptoms appear.

When to escalate

Persistent sneezing, nasal discharge, lethargy, or difficulty breathing → 🔵.

Ear Mites
🟡 Contact board for assistance

Signs to look for

Dark brown to black crumbly or coffee-ground-like buildup in ears. Ferret shaking head frequently, scratching at ears.

What to do

Contact board member immediately — do not attempt to treat without guidance. Treatment typically involves prescription medication (ivermectin or selamectin). All ferrets in contact must be treated, even those without visible symptoms.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — board member will arrange appropriate treatment through our vet relationship.

When to escalate

If ferret's ear is clearly painful, very swollen, or you suspect infection → 🔵 Schedule vet exam.

Mast Cell Tumor — Ulcerated or Growing
🟡 Contact board for assistance

Signs to look for

Previously documented mast cell tumor that has opened, is bleeding, or has grown significantly.

What to do

Keep the area clean. Do not pick at or squeeze the tumor. Board member will assess in person and coordinate with vet for possible cryotherapy or surgical removal.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — board member should see it in person.

When to escalate

Active bleeding that won't stop, signs of systemic infection (fever, lethargy) → 🔴.

Early Adrenal Disease Signs
🟡 Contact board for assistance

Signs to look for

Gradual hair loss (often starting at the tail), pot-bellied appearance, vulvar swelling in spayed females, increased itchiness, muscle loss. Usually develops slowly.

What to do

This is very common in domestic US ferrets and is not an immediate emergency. Document the symptoms with photos and dates. Board member will assess and coordinate a vet appointment to confirm diagnosis and discuss deslorelin implant (Suprelorin) or other management.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — board member will make the vet appointment.

When to escalate

Adrenal disease alone is rarely an emergency unless combined with severe anemia or other crisis signs.

Weight Loss (Gradual)
🟡 Contact board for assistance

Signs to look for

Ferret visibly thinner over days to weeks. You can feel spine or ribs more prominently. Still eating but possibly less than usual.

What to do

Offer highly palatable food (chicken baby food, warm kibble soup). Weigh ferret if scale available. Document and contact board member. Gradual weight loss often signals insulinoma, adrenal disease, or dental pain.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — board member will assess and likely schedule a vet appointment.

When to escalate

Rapid weight loss, refusal to eat, or combined with other symptoms → 🔵 or 🔴.

Dental Disease / Broken Tooth
🟡 Contact board for assistance

Signs to look for

Visible broken or discolored tooth, bad breath, excessive drooling unrelated to low blood sugar, reluctance to eat hard kibble.

What to do

Soften food immediately — soak kibble in warm water or offer chicken baby food. Contact board member. Note that pawing at the mouth can also indicate nausea from low blood sugar — rule that out first.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — board member will coordinate a vet dental exam.

When to escalate

If ferret stops eating entirely or is in clear pain → 🔵 or sooner.

Insulinoma — Confirmed Diagnosis, Medical Management
🔵 Vet appt — contact board to schedule

Signs to look for

Ferret with known insulinoma having more frequent episodes or appearing to need medication adjustment. Current management no longer keeping symptoms controlled.

What to do

Do not adjust medications without vet guidance. Manage episodes at home per the green card. Contact board member to schedule a glucose check and medication review.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — contact the board. We will arrange a time slot with Avon Lake Animal Clinic for you.

When to escalate

Increasing seizure frequency or unresponsive episodes → 🔴.

Bladder Stones / Urinary Issues
🔵 Vet appt — contact board to schedule

Signs to look for

Visiting litter box frequently with little output, straining, crying out when urinating, blood-tinged urine.

What to do

Monitor closely. Ensure fresh water is always available. Contact board member immediately — urinary issues can escalate very quickly, especially in males.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — contact board immediately. We will arrange an urgent appointment with Avon Lake Animal Clinic.

⚠️ Male ferrets with urinary blockage can die within hours. If straining with NO urine output → 🔴 Emergency now.

When to escalate

No urine output + straining → 🔴 Emergency immediately. Do not wait.

Lymphoma Suspicion
🔵 Vet appt — contact board to schedule

Signs to look for

Enlarged lymph nodes (lumps under jaw, armpit, groin), significant unexplained weight loss, chronic lethargy, enlarged spleen detectable on palpation.

What to do

Lymphoma is one of the most common cancers in ferrets. Contact board member right away. Vet evaluation with bloodwork and possibly ultrasound is needed for diagnosis.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — contact the board. We will arrange a time slot with Avon Lake Animal Clinic for you.

When to escalate

Severe breathing difficulty or sudden collapse → 🔴.

Heart Disease Signs
🔵 Vet appt — contact board to schedule

Signs to look for

Hind leg weakness without low blood sugar, coughing, labored breathing, distended abdomen, exercise intolerance.

What to do

Ferrets over 3 years are at risk for cardiomyopathy. Contact board member. Avoid stress and excitement. Vet will need ECG/echocardiogram for diagnosis.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — contact the board. We will arrange a time slot with Avon Lake Animal Clinic for you.

When to escalate

Severe breathing difficulty, open-mouth breathing, or blue-tinged gums → 🔴 Emergency immediately.

Persistent or Recurring Diarrhea (ECE / IBD)
🔵 Vet appt — contact board to schedule

Signs to look for

Recurring diarrhea, green or yellow mucusy stool, 'birdseed' texture poop (grainy), significant weight loss, lethargy.

What to do

Epizootic Catarrhal Enteritis (green slime disease) and IBD are common in ferrets. Supportive care — keep hydrated, offer soft food. Board member should see the ferret and coordinate vet care.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — contact the board. We will arrange a time slot with Avon Lake Animal Clinic for you.

When to escalate

Rapid weight loss, dehydration, or bloody stool → 🔴.

New Unidentified Lump or Mass
🔵 Vet appt — contact board to schedule

Signs to look for

Any new lump or mass you cannot identify as a known mast cell tumor.

What to do

Do not squeeze or attempt to drain. Take a photo. Contact board member. Common benign causes in ferrets include mast cell tumors, basal cell tumors, and cysts — but all new masses need vet evaluation to confirm.

Contact board to arrange

Yes — send a photo immediately.

When to escalate

Rapidly growing, ulcerated, or causing the ferret distress → bump to sooner.

Severe Hypoglycemic Seizure / Unconsciousness
🔴 Emergency vet — go now

Signs to look for

Full body seizure, convulsing, unconscious or will not wake up, lips/gums pale or bluish.

What to do

Rub honey or Karo syrup on gums immediately — do NOT pour liquid into mouth if unconscious (aspiration risk). Keep ferret warm and on its side. Leave for emergency vet NOW. Call while driving.

Board contact

Call board while in transit — do not delay leaving.

🚨 Do not wait. Prolonged hypoglycemia causes irreversible brain damage.
Complete Urinary Blockage (No Urine Output)
🔴 Emergency vet — go now

Signs to look for

Ferret straining in litter box repeatedly with zero urine output. May be crying out. Abdomen may feel hard.

What to do

This is a life-threatening emergency. Go to the vet immediately. Male ferrets are especially at risk. Do not delay to try home remedies.

Board contact

Call while driving.

🚨 Ferrets can die from a complete urinary blockage within hours.
Severe Difficulty Breathing
🔴 Emergency vet — go now

Signs to look for

Open-mouth breathing, rapid or labored breaths, blue or grey gums or tongue, wheezing at rest.

What to do

Keep the ferret calm and cool. Do not restrain tightly. Go to emergency vet immediately.

Board contact

Call while driving.

🚨 Respiratory distress is always an emergency.
GI Obstruction / Swallowed Object
🔴 Emergency vet — go now

Signs to look for

Repeated vomiting or dry heaving, not passing stool, painful abdomen, lethargy, known exposure to foam rubber, rubber, or plastic objects.

What to do

Do not try to induce vomiting. Go to emergency vet immediately. GI obstruction requires surgery. Ferrets love to chew and swallow rubber, foam, and fabric — this is a common killer in young ferrets.

Board contact

Call while driving.

🚨 Ferrets can deteriorate rapidly from GI blockage. Do not wait.
Suspected Canine Distemper
🔴 Emergency vet — go now

Signs to look for

Rash on chin or groin, crusty eyes and nose, extreme lethargy, neurological symptoms. Ferret may have had unverified vaccination status.

What to do

Isolate the ferret immediately from all other animals in the home (dogs and other ferrets are susceptible). Go to the vet. Distemper is almost always fatal in ferrets. Confirm vaccination status with the board.

Board contact

Call immediately — other animals in foster home may be at risk.

🚨 Canine distemper is fatal in ferrets and spreads to dogs and other mustelids. Isolate immediately.
Trauma (Fall, Bite, Crushing Injury)
🔴 Emergency vet — go now

Signs to look for

Ferret was dropped, stepped on, bitten by another animal, or fell from height. May be limping, crying, or non-weight-bearing.

What to do

Do not try to splint or manipulate injured limb. Keep ferret as still as possible in a small, warm carrier. Go to emergency vet.

Board contact

Call while driving.

🚨 Internal injuries may not be visible. Even if the ferret seems okay, get evaluated.
Toxin Ingestion / Poisoning
🔴 Emergency vet — go now

Signs to look for

Ferret ingested a household chemical, plant, medication, or human food known to be toxic. May be drooling excessively, seizing, or lethargic.

What to do

Bring the substance container or plant to the vet. Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed by a vet. Call ASPCA Animal Poison Control (888-426-4435) if vet is unavailable.

Board contact

Call while driving.

🚨 Common ferret hazards: ibuprofen, Tylenol, xylitol, raisins, grapes, chocolate, many houseplants.
Heat Stroke
🔴 Emergency vet — go now

Signs to look for

Ferret is limp, unresponsive, panting (unusual in ferrets), or extremely lethargic in hot conditions. Ferrets cannot tolerate temperatures above ~80°F.

What to do

Move to a cool area immediately. Apply cool (not cold) wet cloths to paws and armpits. Do NOT submerge in cold water — can cause shock. Fan gently. Go to emergency vet.

Board contact

Call while driving.

🚨 Heat stroke in ferrets is rapidly fatal. Ferrets do not pant to cool down like dogs.
